A jitterbug contest in Deep River, Ontario

The film opens with a young Diane winning a Jitterbug contest. Her grandparents are there, beaming. We're rushing forward in first-person POV, and we fall onto a pillow.

Though at first watch you're not aware, everything that follows for the next 95 minutes is a fraction of imagination.

"We Don't Stop here"

A dark-haired woman is in the back seat. The driver pulls over and points a gun at her. man pulls a gun. Then headlights — a car of teenagers racing the wrong way, a head-on collision. The brunette stumbles out, alive but hurt. She walks down into the city.

00:05:30 · Scene 3

A random man tells his friend about a recurring dream

At Winkie's diner, Dan describes the dream he keeps having: a man with a horrible face, behind the building. Herb takes him outside to confront it. The face appears out of nowhere, and Dan collapses. What does this mean? 

00:10:00 · Scene 5

A blonde woman arrives at LAX

Betty Elms steps off a plane from Deep River, Ontario to become an LA actress. On the flight, she meets an older couple. They wave goodbye and burst into private, manic laughter as she walks away. Are they evil?

00:18:30 · Scene 9

Betty finds Rita in her apartment

Betty arrives at Aunt Ruth's beautiful apartment on Havenhurst. The manager, Coco, welcomes her. Inside, Betty discovers a naked, frightened woman in the shower - It's the brunette in the accident, and she has amnesia. She gives herself the name Rita, after a poster on the wall.

00:25:00 · Scene 10

"This is the girl!"

Cut to director Adam Kesher, who meets two mob brothers. They show him a headshot of the girl they want to cast for his film. Adam refuses. The older brother spits espresso in disgust. 

00:43:00 · Scene 17

A cowboy in a corral past midnight

After the casting, Adam is summoned to a high mountain corral by a stranger cowboy. "You will see me one more time if you do good. You will see me two more times if you do bad." Adam is told to cast a different girl: "This is the girl."

00:52:00 · Scene 19

The first audition

Betty performs a scene with an older actor. The script is melodramatic, but Betty makes it terrifying. Everyone in the room is stunned; they think she's a star.

01:06:00 · Scene 22

A body on a bed

Following a clue from Rita's purse, Betty and Rita break into APT17. They find a woman dead on the bed. Rita panics and changes her identity, cuts off her dark hair, and puts on a blonde wig. She looks just like Betty now.

01:10:00 · Scene 23

"I love you"

Betty and Rita are now intimate. Betty whispers, "I'm in love with you." Rita doesn't say it back. It's pitch black. Rita murmurs Spanish words in her sleep — "Silencio… silencio… no hay banda…" She wakes Betty up. They have to drive somewhere now!

02:00:00 · Club Silencio

No hay banda. There is no band. It is all an illusion.

The girls arrive at a theatre and take a seat. A magician on a velvet stage explains, in three languages, that what you've been watching as a viewer isn't real. Betty starts shaking. A woman comes out and sings Llorando until she collapses.  The recording keeps playing...

02:01:30 · Aunt Ruth's Apartment

The blue box (Rita's clue) opens. Betty is gone. The dreamer wakes.

Diane Selwyn who looks just like Betty opens her eyes on a couch in a different apartment. There's an ashtray. There's a coffee cup. There's a blue key on the table.

Part I — continued

what really happened.

Diane is awake; there is no Betty. The next 50 minutes are her memory of the weeks that led up to this moment, broken into flashbacks, in no particular order. We are left to assemble the film chronology on our own. 

02:01:30 · Scene 26

Diane Selwyn wakes up

This is the woman from the bed. Alive. She's been asleep on a robe-strewn couch. Her neighbor knocks — comes to swap apartments back, picks up some lamps, gives Diane a look that says everything has gone wrong here.

02:06:00 · Scene 28 — flashback

On the set, Camilla is the star

Months earlier. Camilla Rhodes (Rita) is shooting a scene with Adam Kesher (directing, joking, kissing her between takes). Diane is in the background as an extra. Adam claps for a closed set; everyone but Diane, Camilla, and Adam is dismissed. Diane lingers a moment too long.

02:10:00 · Scene 29 — flashback

"It's him, isn't it?"

Camilla and Diane are intimate, but the former pulls away while the latter begs. Camilla leaves. The robe and the ashtray and the coffee cup are already there, exactly as they'll be when Diane wakes up.

02:16:00 · Scene 33 — flashback

"We Doon't Stop here"

Diane drives on the same hill Rita was threatened, and she is greeted by Camilla. They walk up the hill to Adam's house. He announces their engagement. Diane laughs along until she bursts into tears. 

02:19:00 · Scene 34 — flashback

A photo. An envelope of cash. A blue key. Same things in Rita's purse. 

Winkie's. Same booth, same waitress (now her nametag reads Betty). Diane slides a photo across the table. "This is the girl!" she tells the hitman. He says she'll find a blue key when it's done. A man at the cash register glances over. Diane stares back.

02:21:00 · Scene 35

The key is on the table

Back to the present. Diane sees the blue key on the coffee table. The deed is done. The grief and guilt land at the same instant.

02:22:00 · Scene 36

The grandparents return screaming

The smiling old couple from the airport, miniature now, crawls out from under the door. Diane runs to her bedroom, pulls a gun from the drawer, and ends it. The face from behind Winkie's appears in smoke.

02:25:00 · Scene 37

Silencio.

A blue-haired woman in the balcony at Club Silencio whispers it. The rest is silence.

PART II — DATA ANALYSIS

Lynch created this puzzle on purpose.

Now that you've seen how the film plays out, you can understand the mapped data underneath. There are statistics behind the difference between dreams and reality. 

Two timelines, one film

These are the 37 scenes laid end-to-end on the runtime. I mapped each block's width to the actual screen time. Hover over any block for the title and timecode. Click for more details.

All 37 scenes are in the order Lynch presents them. The longest scenes run over six minutes, the shortest under thirty seconds.

The reality blocks dim back. What's left bright is the dream (77% of the runtime). The film spends roughly 1h 53m in the dream before Club Silencio breaks the reality. 

The track at the bottom represents the chronological mapping of the real events and when they actually happened. Only twelve of the thirty-seven scenes have a position there. Lines connect each anchored scene to where it really fits.

The criss-cross is the structure. Everything still floating up top with no line down represents only dreams. The film makes you assemble the events in their real order by yourself. 

A river of color

4,394 frames were sampled every 2 seconds. Each vertical bar is the dominant color of a moment in the film, in order.

The dream half holds warm pinks, peaches, and soft yellows: Betty's apartment, the audition stage, the Sylvia North set.

Around minute 113 the saturation collapses. Browns. Reds. Blacks. The river of colors never recovers its earlier warmth.

The mean saturation drops measurably: dream 0.27, reality 0.18. The mean brightness drops as well.

Two faces, one Diane

Each row is one actress, one hundred squares. One square equals 1% of the film's runtime (88 seconds). Cells fill with the dominant color of the scene they cover; a blue inset marks the dream persona, while red represents the reality persona.

The top row is Naomi Watts,  Betty Elms (bright, capable, in love). By the midpoint, she already owns half the runtime.

About 77 squares of the film are dream (1h 53m). The bottom row is the same arithmetic for Laura Harring, mirrored, Rita (highlighted in the same color)

Then Club Silencio and the blue box open, transporting us into the real world, breaking our perspective of the action. The dream is over, the characters change, but not the actors. 

Diane fills the rest of the top row. Camilla flickers in only thirteen squares at the bottom. Reality is shorter, redder, and emptier Hover over any cell you hover or click for more details. 

The breadcrumbs Lynch left (signs)

Thirty motifs recur across the film. Most of them: coffee cup, ashtray, robe, lampshade, espresso, blue key, blue box, belong to both planes, real and dream.  They are the anchor between the two worlds.

The blue key first appears in the dream at minute 50 in Rita's bag. No story behind, no lock to fit. 

It reappears on Diane's coffee table in reality. Now it has a meaning. The key confirms Camilla's death and Diane's actions. 

Every measurable signal flips

This chart layers four time-aligned signals across the 147-minute runtime: shot length, color saturation, audio energy, and silence.

Through the dream, average shot length holds steady around 6.5 seconds. Audio energy stays high. Saturation stays warm.

At minute 113 — the moment Betty disappears — every line fractures within seconds of each other.

Silence climbs. Saturation drops. Cuts shorten. The film stops behaving like the film it was.

Rewatching with knowledge

On a second watch, every dream scene is haunted by what comes after. Same images, different weight.

The bum behind Winkie's. The cowboy. The Castigliane brothers. None of them is about what they seemed to be about; they are all symbols of our fears and anxieties.

Lynch made a film that you can only see clearly, or not, the second time. The data was always there under your nose.

Conclusion

The film is a dream that gets corrected by data.

Mulholland Drive looks chaotic the first time through because Lynch withholds the order in which events actually happened, but on a second, third, or hundredth rewatch, plus a bit of data analysis, a rough interpretation of the movie's theme can be identified. The camera, the colors, the cuts, and the silences are all telling a coherent story underneath. They draw a clean line down the middle of the film. On one side of that line: warmer, slower, more saturated, more spoken. On the other: faster cuts on shorter takes, more silence, less color, less hope.

Lynch manages to trick the viewers with a dream-like world and break their trust, and mess with their minds during the final act of the movie, leaving us with more questions than answers.

What the data shows that a single watch misses

  • Of 37 scenes, only 12 have a place in chronological reality. The other 25 are dream.
  • The dream half runs ~95 minutes; reality only ~50.
  • Mean color saturation falls from 0.27 (dream) to 0.18 (reality).
  • Naomi Watts's dream persona (Betty) gets 18 scenes; her real persona (Diane) gets 11.
  • Laura Harring's dream persona (Rita) gets 16 scenes; her real persona (Camilla) gets 5.
  • Across 2,557 measured shots, the average shot length is 6.5s, but the standard deviation more than doubles in reality, with both very short and very long takes appearing as the editor stops modulating rhythm.

HYPOTHESIS & ROUGH ANALYSIS

Mulholland Drive's dream part is consistently more cinematic than its reality equivalent on every measurable axis: longer takes, warmer color, more sound, more dialogue, more screen time for the lead. Lynch is not just depicting a dream; he is using the formal vocabulary of glamorous studio filmmaking to render it. Reality, by contrast, is shot more like a documentary: shorter, quieter, dimmer, less inhabited. The film's emotional thesis, that Diane dreamed of a hopeful romance action-drama to justify her actions before taking her life, can be deciphered from all of the technical data compiled. However, even with all the data analysis from above, you can still find new meaning in Diane's actions every time you rewatch a movie.

Now that you scrolled this far, I suggest you watch Mulholland Drive again and see the glamorous world of LA and the harsh reality of a mundane actor's life through a different perspective.
